The Urban Transformation of Phnom Penh


For the final project, I decided to demonstrate my hometown city and the place of my research interest through what I have learned in the class. I want to look at a very basic level of how Phnom Penh, the capital city of Cambodia, has changed over the years. With very limited GIS and official census data existed, I have gathered a few simple types of data in this webpage from both local and NGOs website. Below is a very simple image comparing how the city has changed in the span of less than 15 years.

The HighChart below shows the total urban population of the country in comparison with the population of Phnom Penh. Since it is the capital city, the city population constitutes a majority of the whole urban population. There was a huge drop in both population during the 1970s due to the aftermath of the Vietnam war, which resulted in many conflicts and civil wars in Cambodia. Since then, the population has steadily climbing up with more development happening in the city. The population of Phnom Penh today is well above 2 million, and the total urban population of the whole country is only around 3.5 million.




Like most developing countries, development always come with several urban and poverty issues, land disputes, and infrastructure shortage in the city. With the Phnom Penh being one of the fastest growing city in Asia, this presents a whole other sets of social and technical problems. The infrastructure system of the city simply could not cope with such rapid growth, since the city was originally designed to host no more than half a million population, a quarter of what it is now. There have been several attempts to try to control the growth pattern of the city, but not much has been accomplished so far. The city and the government occasionally issued a loose draft of a "master plan" of the city, but very little of the plan has actually been implemented. The image below shows the latest attempt to carry out a master plan for the city for the year 2035, but the validity and effectiveness of the plan are highly questionable.
